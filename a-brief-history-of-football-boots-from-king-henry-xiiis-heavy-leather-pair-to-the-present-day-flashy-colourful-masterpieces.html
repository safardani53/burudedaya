A brief history of football boots, from King Henry XIII's heavy leather pair to the present day flas

FOOTBALL boots have come a long way in the past decade or so, with flasher styles and colours coming into the mainstream.

Add to that the impact scientific advances have had on the footwear and it all points towards just how important boots are in the modern game.

Many manufacturers tweak their boots to help with agility or speed or balance or any other number of important footballing attributes.

But what about back in the 1950s? Or even the 19th century? How about the 16th century?

That's right, there's even records of the leather footwear circulating during the 1500s...

With that in mind, SunSport runs through the history of football boots - from the very start, to the present day.

1526: A royal pair

BELIEVE it or not, the earliest record of football boots dates all the way back to 1526.

King Henry VIII was on the throne at the time - and on his list within the so-called 'Great Wardrobe' was a pair of boots.

They were designed and put together by royal shoemaker Cornelius Johnson for the sum of four shillings - around Â£100 in today's money.

Unfortunately there is no example left in the world - however they were described as being ankle high, made of thick leather and extremely heavy.

I wonder how Cristiano Ronaldo, Lionel Messi and Co would cope in them...

1800s: A steely show

DURING the 19th century, football was still predominately an amateur sport, a hobby and little more - at least, until the very end of the 1800s.

To begin with, would-be heroes would wear their work boots, often with tacks hammered into the soles as makeshift studs, and a steel toe cap.

Imagine getting a whack in the leg with one of those...

As the sport became more mainstream and ultimately professional, a 'slipper' style was introduced to replace the standard high-cut boot - with new rules stating all studs must be rounded leather.

As the years went on, the ankle high style remained to increase protection, while the leather - when wet - would double in weight.

Imagine playing Stoke on a cold Wednesday night in those.

1900 - 1940: Studs-up

WITH football stars suddenly earning a wage and the popularity blossoming, boots were becoming more and more fashionable.

In fact, companies were springing up specifically creating the footwear - complete with replaceable studs for the various weather conditions.

Hugely innovative for its day - but after all, the unpredictability of the British weather almost insisted on it...

1940 - 1960: adidas v Puma

WITH the Second World War over, air travel became much cheaper and international fixtures became commonplace.

Suddenly Europe was introduced to a new, lightweight style of boot - created by the fleet-of-foot South Americans.

And in 1948, German shoemaker Adolf Dassler fell out with his business partner - and brother - Rudolph and formed his own company, which he named adidas.

Meanwhile, Rudolph went on to found Puma in the same year - and two major boot brands were suddenly competing for custom, only furthering the advancements of the footwear.

1960 - 1980: The power of science

THE technological advancements in football boots during the 1960s were huge - with the game increasingly more popular, especially in England following their 1966 World Cup heroics.

Suddenly brands became fashionable, as well as simply useful, as Pele donned Puma boots in the 1962 World Cup.

Four years later and 75 per cent of players were wearing adidas.

With popularity booming, Mitre, Joma and Asics all kicked-off their manufacturing of the footwear.

The birth of the iconic Puma King series with Pele once again at the head of it all.

This all happened during the era of player sponsorship - with certain boot companies jumping on the bandwagon, signing up the biggest name in the sport.

With the advances in boots, suddenly lighter boots were a hit - and white was the black as companies realised, the dark colour wasn't the only option.

Adidas also launched their Copa Mundial and Predator ranges in this era, while Diadora, Umbro and Lotto also came into fruition.

Certainly more than just a piece of leather now, with the world and his wife looking for a slice of the action...

1990s: The Predator

NOW, the adidas Predator boots already existed at this stage, but the 90s was where it really came into its own.

The revolutionary design, style, colour and technology was an instant hit with professionals and Sunday League heroes alike.

The more flexible boot also saw the introduction of a blade option - replacing the century-old stud.

From there, adidas battled with Mizuno, Reebok and Uhlsport for the ultimate boot.

But joining the party as late as the 1990s? Sporting giant Nike...

Nike introduced its iconic Mercurial range, weighing a tiny 200g - compared to its rivals who were still languishing around the 500g mark.

2000 - Present: More than just a boot

THESE days it's clear: Football boots are more than just a simple piece of footwear.

They're stylish, colourful, safe, yet still built for purpose.

The improvement has been mind-boggling - with boots getting lighter still, more like a second-skin these days, safer around the ankle and fine-tuned in every way possible.

And to think, it all started with King Henry VIII...
